Experiment No. 7: Double Refraction (Birefringence)

AIM - APPARATUS - PRIOR CONCEPTS

Aim:To determine the refractive index of ordinary and extra-ordinary rays and identification of types of crystal using the double refraction phenomenon.

Apparatus: Spectrometer, source of light (sodium light), quartz prism & polarizer attachments to the telescope.

Prior Concepts: Refraction, double refraction, polarization, Snell's law of refraction.

THEORY

Birefringence is the optical property of a material having a refractive index that depends on the polarization and propagation direction of light, whereby a ray of light, when incident upon a birefringent material, is split by polarization into two rays taking slightly different paths. When a beam of ordinary light i.e. unpolarised light is incident upon a crystal-like calcite or quartz, then upon entering the crystal, the beam splits into two components both of which are plane polarized with their planes of vibrations mutually perpendicular. Thus, corresponding to a single incident ray, there are two refracted rays. The phenomenon is called double refraction or birefringence & the crystal exhibiting this phenomenon is called double refracting crystal.

For example: Double refraction in calcite crystal l.e. Calcium Carbonate (CaCO3): The direction in which the ray of transmitted light does not suffer double refraction inside the crystal is known as the optic axis. One of these beams is found to obey Snell's law of refraction at the crystal Interface & has vibrations perpendicular to the principal section. This beam is known as ordinary ray or O ray. The second beam does not obey Snell's law and has vibrations parallel to the principal section. This beam or ray is called extra ordinary ray or the E ray. The path of an Incident ray is split into two rays, called O-rays and E-rays. The velocities of O-ray and E-ray are the same along optic axis. The velocity of E-ray is less than that of O-ray in quartz crystal, this is a positive crystal.
